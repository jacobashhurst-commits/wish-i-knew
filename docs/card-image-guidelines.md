# Wish I Knew Image And Illustration Style Guide

Use this guide for all card images, hero illustrations, onboarding artwork, empty states, Weekly Lookahead artwork, timeline visuals and future image generation prompts for Wish I Knew.

Images are not decoration. They are core to the product experience.

Rule: **No picture, no published card.**

## Core Direction

The visual style should feel like a playful Australian parenting adventure map, with warm Saturday-morning-coffee energy.

The image style should combine:

- cosy indie-game charm
- soft pixel-inspired detail
- modern app polish
- Australian coastal and suburban warmth
- collectible card delight
- calm parenting reassurance

The artwork should never feel generic, clinical, corporate, American or beige.

## Inspiration Boundaries

Use these as high-level mood references only:

- warmth, charm and adventure-map feeling from cosy indie games
- collectible object feeling from card-based games
- clean confidence from modern neo-bank apps
- Australian family life
- NSW coastal and suburban mornings
- weekend coffee rituals
- parenting little discoveries

Do not copy Dave the Diver, Up Bank, Pokemon, Bluey or any protected game, brand, character, UI or card layout.

The product needs an original visual identity.

## Default World: NSW / Australian Family Life

For MVP, the default visual flavour is NSW-inspired because the founder lives in NSW.

Use subtle Australian and NSW cues:

- suburban cafes
- weekend coffee cups

- childcare backpacks

- rainy-day living rooms
- beach towels
- picnic rugs
- jacaranda accents

## Illustration Style

Wish I Knew uses a deliberate two-tier visual system. Both tiers share the same palette, warmth and Australian soul, but they serve different jobs.

### Tier 1: Card item art (cute 8-bit pixel collectibles)

This is the default for card images, card thumbnails and Weekly Lookahead row art.

Each card image is a **cute, fun 8-bit / 16-bit pixel-art "item"** — the kind of charming collectible object you'd pick up in a cosy modern pixel game. This is the look the founder asked for explicitly.

Use:

- clearly pixelated, chunky pixels with clean dark outlines
- one or a small group of expressive objects, centred like a collectible item
- subtle dithering and soft pixel shading
- a soft drop shadow under the item
- small sparkle, glow or "level-up" pedestal accents where it fits the moment
- tiny Australian / coastal easter eggs (a starfish, shell, seaweed, gum leaf) when natural
- warm cream background so the item pops

Avoid:

- harsh, ugly or overly noisy low-fi pixel soup
- scary or clinical objects (no scary needles; use a friendly bandage instead)
- text, numbers or UI inside the art
- copying any real game's exact items, characters or layouts

### Tier 2: Hero and scene art (rich illustration)

Hero images, landing/onboarding artwork and large emotional scenes stay in a **richer, painterly illustrated style** (warm, soft-edged, NSW coastal). The coastal hero is the reference. These are not pixel art — they give the brand its lush, calm anchor while the pixel item cards provide the playful, collectible delight.

Shared rules for both tiers: rounded shapes, gentle shadows, warm light, clear silhouettes, bright but controlled colour, original artwork, polished enough for a paid app. Avoid hyper-realism, stock photography, sterile corporate visuals, anime, and Disney-like or Bluey-like styles.

Think: lush illustrated coastal hero, with a deck of cute 8-bit collectible cards inside it.

## Composition Rules

Images must work well inside mobile cards.

Use:

- one clear subject
- simple background
- strong silhouette
- readable object shapes
- low clutter
- warm light
- generous negative space
- image-safe area around key subjects
- friendly details that reward a second look

Avoid:

- tiny important details
- crowded scenes
- too many characters
- text embedded in images
- scary medical imagery
- busy backgrounds
- low-contrast pastel-on-pastel
- overly complex environments

The card title and body sit outside the image, so the image does not need to explain everything by itself.

## Preferred Image Types

### Object Cards

Best for Tiny Gear Shift cards.

Examples: toddler spoon, straw cup, sleep sack, sun hat, daycare backpack, snack container, pram fan, labels, first shoes, bib, high chair.

Style: make the object feel like a little level-up item.

### Scene Cards

Best for Big Milestone, Parent Sanity and Fun First cards.

Examples: parent with coffee checking Weekly Lookahead, messy high chair at breakfast, pram near a beach path, family packing for a first weekend away, childcare bag by the front door, rainy-day sick setup on a kitchen bench.

Style: warm, human, calm and not too busy.

### Symbolic Cards

Best for Admin Trap and Aussie System cards.

Examples: calendar with friendly stickers, Medicare-style card shape without copying official designs, clipboard with blocks and dummy, folder with tiny baby socks, map pin with gum leaf, immunisation appointment card with gentle bandage sticker.

Style: make boring admin feel manageable.

### Emotional Cards

Best for Parent Sanity cards.

Examples: two coffee cups on a messy bench, couch with folded laundry and baby monitor, moonlit cot and tired parent silhouette, quiet morning window with pram nearby, parent sitting with phone and tea.

Style: warm, gentle, human and never preachy.

## Card Image Requirements

Every published card must have:

- `image_url`
- `image_alt`
- `image_status` of `approved` or `uploaded`
- image that matches the card category and tone

Images should support the card's emotional job.

- Big Milestone: make important milestones feel clear and manageable.
- Tiny Gear Shift: make a small practical item feel like a delightful discovery.
- Heads Up: give a gentle warning without fear.
- Admin Trap: make admin feel less painful.
- Parent Sanity: make parents feel seen.
- Aussie System: feel local, trustworthy and practical.
- Fun First: make family life feel enjoyable and possible.

## Aspect Ratios

Prioritise:

- Card image: 4:3 or 3:2 for timeline cards, saved cards and card detail.
- Hero image: 16:9 or wide banner for landing, Weekly Lookahead and onboarding.
- Square icon image: 1:1 for category badges, thumbnails and future widgets.

Source artwork should have enough safe margin to crop responsively.

## Accessibility And Alt Text

Every image must have alt text.

Good alt text:

- Illustration of a toddler spoon with small sparkles on a warm cream background.
- Illustration of a childcare backpack with a sun hat, water bottle and soft toy.
- Warm illustration of a parent drinking coffee while checking a phone beside a pram.

Bad alt text:

- Cute pic.
- Baby stuff.
- Fun image.

Do not put important instructions only inside images. The image supports the card; the text carries the advice.

## Hero Image Direction

The main brand hero should show the product world.

Suggested concept: a warm Saturday morning in coastal NSW. A parent sits with coffee checking the Wish I Knew Weekly Lookahead on their phone. Nearby are a pram, tiny sun hat, childcare backpack, soft toy and playful milestone cards. Through the window or background are subtle NSW cues such as ocean light, gum trees, sandstone cliffs, a coastal path, ocean pool or ferry in the distance.

The hero should communicate:

- Australian
- parenthood
- journey
- fun
- useful
- calm
- weekly ritual

Do not overload the hero with landmarks.

## Weekly Lookahead Image Direction

Weekly Lookahead is the visual heart of the app.

Mood:

- Saturday morning
- coffee
- calm planning
- family rhythm
- warm light
- we have got this
- not urgent
- not clinical

Suggested scene: coffee cup, phone, soft toy and milestone cards on a kitchen table with warm morning light. A pram or baby bag is nearby. The phone shows a simplified timeline/check-in screen. Outside the window are gum leaves or a hint of coastal NSW.

Avoid chaotic baby mess, stressed faces, urgent red alerts, too much phone-screen text or anything that looks like a productivity dashboard.

## Negative Prompt Guidance

Avoid:

- photorealistic babies
- uncanny baby faces
- stock photography
- hospital fear imagery
- close-up needles
- crying distressed babies as the main subject
- cluttered toy explosions
- American suburban imagery
- snow unless state/season relevant
- copyrighted characters
- Pokemon card layout
- Dave the Diver characters or UI
- Up Bank branding or UI
- Bluey-like dog family characters
- text inside the image
- logos
- brand names
- watermarks
- gambling or loot-box visual language

## Placeholder Image Rule

During MVP build, placeholder images are acceptable, but they must still support the visual system.

Do not use random internet images. Do not hotlink external images.

Use app-owned placeholder panels such as:

- warm illustrated gradients with category icons
- simple object silhouettes
- reusable NSW coastal backgrounds
- soft card-type badge images

Even placeholders must preserve image space in the card component. The UI must never assume a card can be text-only.

## Review Checklist

Before approving a card image, check:

- Does it match the card's emotional job?
- Does it feel Australian or locally relevant where appropriate?
- Is it warm and playful without being childish?
- Is it readable at mobile card size?
- Is the subject clear?
- Is it original?
- Does it avoid copyrighted styles?
- Does it avoid scary or clinical imagery?
- Does it have alt text?
- Does it work with the card category colour?
- Would a tired parent enjoy seeing this in their Weekly Lookahead?

If not, revise the image before publishing the card.

## Final Direction

Wish I Knew images should feel like a warm, playful NSW/Australian parenting adventure, delivered through modern illustrated cards with a little pixel-inspired charm.

Images should be fun, useful, calm, collectible-feeling, Australian, mobile-readable, trustworthy and original.

Do not make the app beige. Do not make it clinical. Do not make it childish.

Make the cards feel like small, helpful discoveries parents look forward to unlocking each week.
